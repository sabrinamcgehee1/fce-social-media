Part 2
The Universal Pattern
Despite their differences, all 5 creators use the same core structure. This is the editing formula that works:
The 6-Layer Video Structure
Layer 1 — Your face (the anchor): You talking to camera. This is 70–80% of what's on screen. Clean background, good lighting, eye-level or slightly above.
Layer 2 — B-roll images (the proof): Photos, screenshots, book covers, or lifestyle images that appear while you talk. 3–6 per video. They appear on screen for 2–4 seconds each.
Layer 3 — Key phrase text (the quotable): When you say something important, it appears as text on screen in your signature font. 2–4 per video. These are what people screenshot.
Layer 4 — Subtitles (the accessibility): Auto-generated captions, styled in your brand font, running throughout.
Layer 5 — Background music (the mood): Soft, looping, consistent. Same vibe every video. 15–20% volume.
Layer 6 — Sound effects (the polish): Subtle "pop," "whoosh," or "click" when images or text appear. This is the detail that separates amateur from polished.
Part 3
10 Decisions That Create Your Signature
Your signature editing style comes from making these 10 decisions ONCE and then never changing them. This is what makes people recognize your content in 2 seconds.
1. What's your filming setup?
🪑
Seated at desk (like Nana)
Clean desk, natural light from the side, bookshelf or plant behind you. Works for: productivity, finance, learning content.
🧍‍♀️
Standing/handheld (like Tizi)
More casual, slightly closer to camera, can walk around. Works for: lifestyle, mindset, storytelling content.
☕
Cozy corner (like Isabel)
Bed, couch, or reading nook. Warm lighting, blankets visible. Works for: intimate, journal-style, personal content.
2. Where do your B-roll images come from?
📸
Mostly your own photos (like Isabel)
Your twins, your workspace, your coffee, Brazil vs. US moments. Most personal. Requires building a photo library.
📱
Mostly screenshots + book covers (like Nana/NoteswNat)
Articles, stats, app screenshots, book covers. Most credible. Easier to source — just screenshot as you research.
🎨
Mix of both + Pinterest-quality stock (like Giulia)
Some personal, some screenshots, some curated stock from Unsplash/Canva. Most aesthetic. Requires curation time.
3. How do your B-roll images enter the screen?
➡️
Slide in from the right
Most common among your inspirations. Clean, professional. Image slides in, stays 2–3 seconds, slides out.
⬆️
Pop up from below
Slightly more playful. Image rises from bottom of frame, stays, then drops back down.
✨
Fade in (with slight scale)
Most subtle. Image gently appears (opacity 0→100%) while scaling slightly (95%→100%). Elegant, cinematic.
4. How big are your B-roll images on screen?
🖼️
Small (25–30% of screen) — upper right corner
Your face stays dominant. Image is supporting evidence. Best for: talking-head-focused content.
📐
Medium (35–45% of screen) — right side
Balanced between face and image. Most common size your inspirations use. Best for: most content types.
🖥️
Full takeover (80–100%) for 1–2 seconds
Your face disappears briefly while the image takes over. Returns to face. Dramatic. Best for: big reveals or key stats.
5. What font for key phrases on screen?
✍️
Sedan SC (serif) — elegant, editorial
Closest to Instrument Serif in CapCut. Use for: quotable moments, lesson punchlines. Color: white or sage green.
💪
Montserrat Bold (sans-serif) — clean, punchy
More like Nana's style. Strong, modern, highly readable. Use for: stats, frameworks, action items. Color: white.
6. Where do key phrases appear on screen?
⬆️
Top third (above your head)
Doesn't cover your face. Clean separation between you and the text. Most professional.
↔️
Center (over your chest area)
Maximum visual impact. Text is the star for that moment. Slightly covers you but demands attention.
⬇️
Lower third (subtitle area)
Integrated with your captions. Less dramatic but cleaner. Works if your subtitles are already in the lower third.
7. What's your background music vibe?
🎹
Soft piano / acoustic guitar
Warm, personal, journal-like. Matches P1 (cultural/identity) content well. Isabel's vibe.
🎧
Lo-fi / ambient beats
Modern, productive, focused. Matches P2 (learning/finance) content well. Tizi and Giulia's vibe.
🔇
Very minimal / almost silent
Lets your voice be the star. More serious, research-focused. NoteswNat's vibe.
8. What sound effect when images/text appear?
🫧
Soft "pop" / bubble
Playful, light. Search CapCut SFX: "pop" or "bubble." Good for warm, approachable content.
💨
Subtle "whoosh" / swoosh
Clean, professional. Search CapCut SFX: "whoosh" or "swoosh." Good for polished, editorial content.
📄
Paper / page turn
Unique, journal-like. Search CapCut SFX: "page turn" or "paper." Ties to your 1% Journal brand.
9. How do you cut between sentences?
✂️
Jump cuts (remove all pauses)
Fast, punchy, high-energy. Nana's style. Good for information-dense content. Can feel rushed if overdone.
🫁
Breathing room (keep small pauses)
More natural, calm, intentional. Tizi/Isabel's style. Good for mindset/personal content. Feels more intimate.
10. How does your video end?
🪧
Branded end card (1.5 seconds)
Static image with your name, CTA, and brand colors. Fade transition. Most professional.
🗣️
Verbal CTA on camera
You say "follow for more" or "link in bio" directly to camera. More personal. No separate end card needed.
🔁
Loop back to hook
Last frame visually matches first frame, creating a seamless loop. Increases watch time. Advanced technique.

Part 5
Build Your Template — Step by Step
Open CapCut right now and follow these steps. You'll have a reusable master template in about 30 minutes.
Film a test clip (60 seconds of you talking about anything)
Use natural light from a window to your side. Phone at eye level or slightly above. Clean background. Don't worry about what you say — this is just for building the template.
Import into CapCut → Trim all pauses
Go through the clip and split/delete every pause, "um," and dead space. This is your jump cut pass. Watch it back — it should feel faster than you naturally talk.
Apply your color preset
Select the clip → Adjust → Color Temp: +3, Saturation: -8, Highlights: +5, Shadows: +3, Fade: +10. Save as "Sabrina Clean" preset. This desaturated warm look is the foundation of your aesthetic.
Add background music
CapCut Audio → search "lo-fi" or "soft acoustic" → pick ONE track → trim to match your video length → set volume to 15–20%. This track becomes your permanent sound. Listen to 5–10 options and choose the one that feels most "you."
Generate auto-captions
Text → Auto captions → generate → change font to Montserrat (or closest clean sans-serif) → white text → black background at 30% opacity → centered in the lower third. Save this style.
Add your first B-roll image
Import a test image (any book cover or screenshot) → add as overlay → resize to ~35% of screen → position upper right → add "Slide In Right" animation (0.3s in, 0.3s out) → set duration to 3 seconds. Time it to when you mention the thing the image represents. Add a sound effect: SFX → search "pop" or "whoosh" → place at the exact moment the image appears → volume 40%.
Add a key phrase text overlay
Text → add text → type your quotable line → font: Sedan SC (or Playfair Display) → size: 22–26px → color: white → position: top third of screen → add "Fade In" animation (0.2s) → duration: match spoken phrase length. Optional: add a subtle sage green (#7A8F7A) underline or background highlight behind the text.
Create your end card
In Canva: 1080×1920, Linen (#F2F0ED) background, "follow for more" in Instrument Serif, your @handle in Sora underneath, thin sage green line. Export as PNG. Import into CapCut as the final 1.5 seconds with a 0.3s fade transition.
Duplicate the image/text pattern
Now add 2–3 more B-roll images and 1–2 more text overlays, following the same settings from Steps 6–7. Each image gets its own SFX pop. Space them evenly throughout the video — roughly one every 8–12 seconds.
Save as "Sabrina Master" and NEVER delete it
This project is your reusable template. For every new video: duplicate this project → swap the main clip → replace B-roll images → update text overlays → export. Your editing time drops from 45+ minutes to 15–20 minutes per video.
